ChopStix

The chopstick device consists of a hardware and software component. The hardware comonent includes a circuit board and the 3D printed chopticks.

1. Design requirements
For the choptick device to work, it needs to detect electric signals from a flexed muscle. The circuit procresses the electrical signal and the code uses this information to open and close the chopsticks. It was decided that only one of the chopsticks would move since the movement would mimic how chopsticks are used by majority of people. To move one of the chopsticks, a 4-bar linkage system was used where a servo horn from a servo motor would act as the crank for the linkage system. The chopstick would act as a rocker of the 4-bar linkage system. Experimentally, it was determined that the chopsticks undergo 25 degrees of separation and 0.5 seconds of time is needed to close the chopstick. This meant that the angular velocity of the chopstick was 50 degrees/second. Simulating the crank with SolidWork's motion analysis feature, the simulation determined that the crank needed to produce an angular velocity of 40 rpm which is 4 N-mm. Since the servo motor would have a range of 180 degrees (half a revolution), it was determined that 40 rpm is 80 half-revolutions/minute which is equivalent to 1 half-rotation/0.75 seconds.

     

2. Mechanism Prototyping
The original design for the device was to attach the machanism to a compression sleeve that the user could wear while using the device. But given the size of the device, it was evident that the compression sleeve wouldn't be able to support the weight of the mechanism. So the mechanism design tranformed into a board that could be held by the user. The chopsticks extruded from the board with one chopstick being mobile. The movable chopstick would be a part of a 4-bar linkage system with the servo motor acting as the crank. A hook and wrist strap have been placed on the back of the machanism so that it can be held by users who have no use motor control of their hands. Grooves have been cut onto the left hand side of the mechanism as an area where users could place their fingers to stabilize the mechanism if they need to.

3. Circuitry
The motion of the chopsticks would be triggered by the EMG signals produced when the bicep is flexed. Various sub components were added to process the EMG signals and to differentiate the actual signal from noise. An instrumentation amplifier was used to amplify the signals produced from the muscle. A low pass and high pass filter were used to filter out any signals that were outside the range of 20-600 Hz since those signals would constitute as noise instead of a proper EMG signal. After the bandpass filter, a full wave rectifier was added to convert the alternating current to a direct current for the arduino. The inverting amplifier was used to invert the signal and a peak detector was used to capture the highest positive points of the signal. Finally a comparator is used to compare the reference signal from the EMG signal to the reference voltage of 3.3 volts and output a 1 to the arduino if the signal is higher than the reference voltage or a 0 if the signal is lower than the reference voltage.

4. Arduino
The arduino is meant to recieve the output signal of the circuit. If the output signal is HIGH then the modulo code implemented into the arduino would cause the chopstick to open and close with a delay of 2000 miliseconds for each open and close action that is completed by the chopstick. This gives the user time to position the chopstick and pick up and object and place it in the desired position. When the output signal is LOW the chopstick stops moving and remains in the position it stopped at.
